Historical Highlights


Meiji Shrine, or Meiji Jingu, is a Shinto shrine located in Shibuya, Tokyo, and is dedicated to the deified spirits of Emperor Meiji and his consort, Empress Shoken. Established in 1920, it commemorates the emperor's role in the Meiji Restoration, which saw the modernization and industrialization of Japan in the late 19th century. The shrine was destroyed during World War II but was rebuilt in 1958, symbolizing Japan's post-war growth and recovery. It stands as a serene and verdant space amidst Tokyo's bustling cityscape, attracting millions of visitors each year, especially during the New Year celebrations.

Visitor Information


Meiji Shrine is nestled within a 170-acre forest that offers a tranquil escape from the city. It is open daily from sunrise to sunset, and there is no admission fee to enter the shrine grounds. Visitors can explore the shrine's inner garden, which has an entrance fee, and enjoy the seasonal beauty of the iris garden in June. The shrine is easily accessible via public transport, with the nearest stations being Harajuku Station on the JR Yamanote Line and Meiji-jingu-mae Station on the Tokyo Metro Chiyoda and Fukutoshin Lines. Cultural events and traditional Shinto weddings are frequent, providing a glimpse into Japanese customs and traditions

Story and Legends


The Meiji Shrine is steeped in cultural and spiritual significance, embodying the souls of Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken. Emperor Meiji is revered for his enlightened rule that transformed Japan into a modern state. Several legends and stories highlight the shrine's sacredness, including those about the divine protection it offers to visitors. One such tale is of the 100,000 trees that make up the surrounding forest, donated from regions all across Japan, symbolizing unity and peace. Another story tells of the shrine surviving the air raids of World War II relatively unscathed, which many believe to be a miracle and testament to its divine guardianship.
